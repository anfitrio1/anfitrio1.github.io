Market structure and proximity to transport infrastructures: evidence from India — Draft

This paper investigates how transport infrastructures shape the local market structure and firms’ market power, bridging the gap between the literature on rising market concentration, trade, and economic geography. My hypothesis is that large firms disproportionately benefit from infrastructure proximity. By locating their production plants near high-speed transport links, these firms reduce transportation costs, reduce their prices, and expand their scale. However, due to imperfect pass-through, they also capture part of these cost savings as higher markups rather than passing them on to consumers. Only larger firms can afford to pay the fixed cost of locating next to the infrastructure, pushing away smaller competitors. I use the improvement of India’s highway network over the first decade of the XXI century as a quasi-natural experiment to test my hypothesis. These findings challenge the traditional "pro-competitive" narrative of infrastructure development, showing that enhanced connectivity can inadvertently consolidate market power among dominant firms and exacerbate spatial inequalities.
